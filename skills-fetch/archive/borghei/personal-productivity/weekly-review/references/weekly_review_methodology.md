# Weekly Review Methodology

The weekly review is the highest-leverage personal-effectiveness habit you can adopt. It's where you convert week-to-week task execution into season-long compounding.

---

## The Core Idea

Without a weekly review:
- Tasks pile up; you never decide what to drop
- OKRs drift quietly until the quarter ends
- You repeat avoidable mistakes
- You over-commit because you never see the full week ahead

A weekly review is where you make the meta-decisions that the moment-to-moment task layer can't.

---

## The Standard Components

### Inputs to gather (5-10 min)

- Calendar from the past week (what actually happened)
- Task list / inbox state (what's open, what's stuck)
- Email / Slack scan (anything pending)
- Journal entries / notes from the week
- OKR / goal scoreboard

### Components to capture (15-20 min)

**Wins** — 3-5 things that went well. Specific. Names and numbers.

**Learnings** — 1-3 things you learned, especially things you didn't want to. What surprised you?

**Blockers / risks** — 1-3 things that could derail next week. Each with an owner (often you) and a mitigation.

**OKR / goal progress** — for each active OKR, where you are and whether the trend supports closing the gap.

**Next-week priorities** — top 3 priorities. Not 10. Not 5. Three.

**Open loops to close** — small annoyances accumulating attention.

**Things to actively decline** — meetings, asks, distractions you'll say no to.

**Journal note** — 1-2 sentences for your future self.

### Self-coach prompts (5 min)

- What did I avoid this week that I shouldn't have?
- What am I telling myself I'm too busy for that I'm actually just avoiding?
- What would I do differently if I started this week over?
- What is the single most important thing for next week?

## The GTD Weekly Review

David Allen's classic version:

1. **Get clear** — collect all loose papers and inputs; process to zero
2. **Get current** — review action lists, calendar, projects, areas of focus
3. **Get creative** — review someday/maybe; capture new ideas

Allen's GTD review is more comprehensive than what most people need; the structure here is a lighter weight version. If GTD resonates, do the full thing.

---

## OKR Check-In

If you use OKRs, the weekly review is the right rhythm for OKR check-ins:

- For each KR, current progress vs. trajectory needed for end-of-quarter
- Identify any KR that has drifted off-track
- Decide: invest more, descope, or accept the miss

Weekly is signal; quarterly is the action moment. Don't churn the OKRs themselves week-to-week.

---

## Cadence

Most people do a weekly review on Friday afternoon or Sunday evening. Both work; consistency matters more than which.

**Friday afternoon:**
- Pro: while context is fresh
- Pro: you go into weekend with a clean mental ledger
- Con: end-of-week energy may be low

**Sunday evening:**
- Pro: better energy after rest
- Pro: helps prime Monday
- Con: weekend brain is slower; some details are foggy

Either works. Pick one. Defend the time.

---

## Length

- **Target:** 30-45 minutes
- **Maximum:** 60 minutes
- **Floor:** 20 minutes (still better than skipping)

If your reviews are taking 90 minutes, you're trying to do too much in the review. Move some of it (deep planning, OKR refresh) to a separate monthly or quarterly session.

---

## Quarterly Pattern Review

Save your weekly reviews in a single folder. Quarterly:

1. Read all 12-13 weeks back-to-back
2. Look for patterns:
   - Recurring blockers you keep mentioning but never fix
   - Energy patterns (which weeks were great? what was different?)
   - OKR drift (which quarters did you over-commit?)
   - Avoidance patterns (what do you keep promising to do "next week"?)
3. Adjust:
   - Re-baseline OKRs if structurally over-committed
   - Schedule a single "fix the recurring blocker" deep work session
   - Decline the meeting category that's draining energy

This compounding-pattern review is where the long-term value of weekly reviews shows up.

---

## Common Pitfalls

- **Skipping when busy.** The weeks you most need to review are the weeks you skip. Don't.
- **Over-formatting.** Pretty reviews you don't read are worse than ugly ones you do.
- **No wins captured.** Always note wins, even when the week was hard. Pattern of wins matters.
- **No declines captured.** A weekly review without explicit "no" is incomplete.
- **Inputs without outputs.** Reflection without a next-week priority list is performance theater.
- **OKR thrash.** Weekly is for check-in, not re-baselining. Resist the urge to change the targets.
- **Brain dumping into the review instead of triaging.** The review consolidates; doesn't replace your task list.

---

## When to Skip

Almost never. But:

- You're on real vacation (no work check-in) — skip the review of those weeks
- Major personal event (illness, family, etc.) — skip and resume
- The act of reviewing is causing more anxiety than benefit — see a coach / therapist; the review isn't the problem
