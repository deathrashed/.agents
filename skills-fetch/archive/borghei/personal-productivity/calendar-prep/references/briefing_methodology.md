# Briefing Methodology

Every meeting deserves a 30-second-or-less mental rehearsal of: **what's the goal, who am I trying to convince of what, and what's my fallback.** A written briefing is that rehearsal made permanent.

---

## When Briefings Help

- **High-stakes meetings** — board, investors, customer renewals, M&A
- **Multi-stakeholder meetings** — when you need to manage three or four agendas in one room
- **Returning meetings** — second / third meetings on the same topic where context drift is likely
- **Pre-meetings before bigger meetings** — alignment-building rounds with key stakeholders

## When Briefings Are Overhead

- **Standups** — too short, too frequent
- **Spontaneous pair work** — destroys the spontaneity
- **Recurring 1:1s with someone you talk to every day** — already aligned

The line: if you'd benefit from re-reading prior context, you'd benefit from a briefing.

---

## What Goes In a Briefing

**The decision** — what specifically are you trying to decide? "Discuss the roadmap" is not a decision; "approve Q3 priorities" is.

**The attendees and their positions** — who's likely to be for, against, neutral. What's their motivation?

**Talking points** — your strongest 3-5 points, ordered by importance.

**Counter-arguments to anticipate** — what objections do you expect, and what's your response?

**Questions to ask** — the things you don't know that you need to find out.

**Metrics** — the numbers that anchor the discussion.

**Success and fallback** — best case + acceptable case + walk-away.

---

## What Doesn't Go In a Briefing

- Long history. Use links, not paragraphs.
- Your full deck. The deck is for the meeting; the briefing is for you.
- Speculation about other attendees' motivations expressed disrespectfully (that briefing will leak).
- Personal commentary on attendees that you wouldn't want repeated.

---

## Length Discipline

- **Target:** 1 page (300-400 words)
- **Maximum:** 1.5 pages
- Beyond that: you'll skim it; the briefing fails its job.

---

## Reading the Briefing

Generate the briefing the day before. Read it 30-60 minutes before the meeting. Re-read the **decisions and fallback** in the 5 minutes before walking in.

If you generate a briefing and don't read it, you've performed the ritual without getting the value.

---

## Common Pitfalls

- **Padding.** Adding sections to feel thorough — brief is the point.
- **No counter-arguments.** Briefings without anticipated counters are wishful thinking.
- **Same template everywhere.** A board briefing is not a 1:1 briefing.
- **Briefing for show.** Writing a briefing the team will see, not for yourself, distorts the writing.
- **No update for second meeting.** Re-using the prior briefing without updating context misleads.

---

## After the Meeting

The briefing's questions should be answered in the meeting's decisions. Pair with `personal-productivity/meeting-insights/` to extract decisions, action items, owners, and dates from the meeting transcript.

If the meeting closed your briefing's open questions, the briefing did its job.
