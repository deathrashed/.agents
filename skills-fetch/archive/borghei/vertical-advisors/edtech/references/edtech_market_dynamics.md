# Edtech Market Dynamics

Edtech contains four distinct markets with different buyers, sales cycles, and economics. Picking the wrong one is one of the most common reasons edtech startups fail despite good products.

---

## The Four Markets

| Market | Buyer | Sales cycle | Contract | ACV |
|--------|-------|-------------|----------|------|
| K-12 (school district) | District CTO / curriculum / superintendent | 3-12 months | Annual or multi-year, per-student | $5-$50 per student/year |
| Higher Ed (institutional) | Provost / CIO / Dept Chair | 6-18 months | Multi-year, license + per-student | $50K-$5M institutional |
| Corporate L&D | CHRO / L&D leader | 3-9 months | Annual subscription, per-seat | $50-$500 per seat/year |
| Direct-to-Learner (D2C) | Individual learner / parent | Days | Subscription or one-time | $10-$500/year |

These are different businesses. Treating them as one is the #1 strategic error.

---

## K-12 (School District)

**Buyer:** US K-12 schools, organized in ~13,000 districts. Districts are the buying unit; schools are the consuming unit.

**Decision-makers:**
- **Superintendent** — final authority for major contracts; rarely the technical buyer
- **Assistant Superintendent / Chief Academic Officer** — curriculum decisions
- **Chief Technology Officer / Director of IT** — infrastructure, integrations, security
- **Director of Curriculum / Instructional Technology** — pedagogy fit
- **Principals** — site-level buy-in
- **Teachers** — usage-driver, sometimes pilot evaluators

**Sales cycle:**
- Pilots common (paid or free)
- Annual budget cycles, calendar-aligned (start fall)
- Procurement office often involved for >$25K-$50K
- RFP for larger purchases

**Pricing:**
- Per-student annual fee, $5-$50 typical for K-12 supplemental tools
- Large LMS / SIS deals: per-student or institutional tiers
- Free + freemium common (sustained by district-paid premium tiers)

**Implementation:**
- Rostering: integrate with Clever / ClassLink for student/class data
- SIS integration: PowerSchool, Infinite Campus, etc.
- LMS integration: Schoology, Canvas, Google Classroom, Microsoft Teams Education
- Professional Development (PD): teacher training is often the bottleneck

**Calendar:**
- **Spring (Feb-May)** — biggest budget decisions for next school year
- **Summer (Jun-Aug)** — implementation, training
- **Fall (Sep-Nov)** — small adjustments
- **Winter (Dec-Jan)** — typically too late to start sales motion for current school year

**ESSER funding:** US $190B Elementary and Secondary School Emergency Relief expired in 2024-2025. Edtech market dynamics have shifted post-ESSER — districts more cost-sensitive, fewer experimental purchases.

**What works in K-12:**
- Teacher-driven adoption (bottoms-up, then enterprise contract)
- Strong rostering and SSO integration day 1
- Outcomes data (test scores, engagement, equity metrics)
- District references in similar demographics

**What doesn't work:**
- Cold outreach to teachers without district awareness (purchase blocked at district level)
- Selling to one school in a district at a time
- Underestimating SDPA / data-privacy obligations

## Higher Education

**Buyer:** Universities, colleges, sometimes individual departments

**Decision-makers vary by purchase type:**
- Institutional tools (LMS, SIS) — Provost, CIO
- Department tools (specific subject software) — Department Chair, Dean
- Faculty research tools — individual faculty + research IT

**Sales cycle:**
- 6-18 months for institutional purchases
- Procurement and IT review can be slow
- Faculty senate / academic affairs may need to weigh in
- Multi-year contracts common

**Pricing:**
- Site licenses common at the institutional level
- Per-student tiers
- Open-access / OER pressure on some categories

**Implementation:**
- LMS integration: Canvas (dominant), Blackboard, D2L Brightspace, Moodle
- SIS integration: Banner, Workday Student, Colleague
- SSO (SAML / OIDC) standard expectation
- Accessibility (ADA / Section 508) usually contractual requirement

**What works in Higher Ed:**
- Faculty champion model (one faculty pilot leads to broader adoption)
- Conferences (EDUCAUSE, AAC&U, subject-specific)
- Strong reference institutions
- Standards compliance (LTI, QTI, IMS Global)

**What doesn't work:**
- Speed-focused sales motions (higher ed is slow by structure)
- Treating all higher ed as one market (R1 universities ≠ regional state schools ≠ community colleges ≠ for-profit)

## Corporate Learning & Development

**Buyer:** Companies, for employee development

**Decision-makers:**
- **Head of L&D** / Chief Learning Officer
- **CHRO** for major / strategic purchases
- **Department heads** for specific functional content (sales enablement, engineering training, etc.)

**Sales cycle:**
- 3-9 months
- Annual L&D budget cycles
- RFP for >$100K typical at enterprise

**Pricing:**
- Per-seat per-year typical: $50-$500 depending on category
- Tiered packages (basic / pro / enterprise)
- Usage-tied pricing (per-completion) less common

**Implementation:**
- LMS integration: Cornerstone, Workday Learning, SAP SuccessFactors, custom
- HRIS integration: Workday, BambooHR, ADP
- SSO standard
- xAPI / SCORM for tracking

**What works in corporate L&D:**
- Tied to specific skills / certifications
- Measurable behavior change post-program
- Strong analytics and reporting for L&D dashboards
- Compliance (security training, sexual harassment, etc.) — required, sticky, easy renewals

**What doesn't work:**
- "Educational philosophy" pitches without business outcome framing
- Long-form courses that don't fit corporate attention budgets

## Direct-to-Learner (D2C)

**Buyer:** Individual learners or parents

**Decision-makers:** The individual

**Sales cycle:** Days

**Pricing:** Subscription ($10-$50/month typical) or one-time (Coursera-like courses $50-$500)

**Categories:**
- Test prep (SAT, GRE, language certifications)
- Skills (coding bootcamps, design, marketing)
- Languages (Duolingo, Babbel, Pimsleur)
- Hobbies / lifelong learning (MasterClass)
- Children's learning supplements (parents buying for kids)

**What works in D2C:**
- Specific outcome focus (pass test, get job, achieve fluency)
- Strong brand and content marketing
- Community and social features
- Tight CAC payback economics

**What doesn't work:**
- Generic "educational platform" without category specificity
- Free-only models in markets where users won't pay
- Long content cycles when learners want quick wins

## Common Mistakes

1. **Targeting all four markets.** Each requires different sales motion, content strategy, pricing. Pick one.
2. **Underestimating procurement time.** Especially K-12 and higher-ed: months of paperwork after sales close.
3. **Wrong calendar.** Selling K-12 in October to start in November doesn't work; selling D2C in summer when learners take breaks doesn't work.
4. **One product for everyone.** K-12 product != higher ed product != corporate product, even if the underlying tech is the same.
5. **Underweighting compliance.** Edtech compliance (FERPA, COPPA, state laws, SDPAs) is operationally heavy. Plan it in product, not after.
6. **Pricing without market reference.** Edtech pricing varies wildly by segment; getting it 5x wrong is common.
