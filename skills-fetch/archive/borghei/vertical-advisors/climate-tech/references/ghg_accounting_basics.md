# GHG Accounting Basics

Climate-tech founders need to understand GHG (greenhouse gas) accounting to talk credibly with customers, investors, and verifiers. This is orientation; rigorous GHG accounting requires methodology selection and qualified practitioners.

---

## The GHG Protocol

The de facto international standard for GHG accounting is the GHG Protocol (ghgprotocol.org). It defines:

- **Scope 1:** Direct emissions from owned/controlled sources (combustion in company-owned vehicles, on-site fuel use, process emissions from owned facilities, refrigerant leaks)
- **Scope 2:** Indirect emissions from purchased electricity, steam, heating, cooling
- **Scope 3:** All other indirect emissions in the value chain — upstream (suppliers, business travel, employee commute) and downstream (use of sold products, end-of-life)

Most company-level emissions inventories follow GHG Protocol. ISO 14064 is a compatible standard with some differences.

---

## Scope 3 Categories

GHG Protocol defines 15 Scope 3 categories:

**Upstream:**
1. Purchased goods and services
2. Capital goods
3. Fuel and energy not in Scope 1/2
4. Upstream transportation and distribution
5. Waste in operations
6. Business travel
7. Employee commuting
8. Upstream leased assets

**Downstream:**
9. Downstream transportation and distribution
10. Processing of sold products
11. Use of sold products
12. End-of-life of sold products
13. Downstream leased assets
14. Franchises
15. Investments

**Most companies' largest emissions are Scope 3** — typically purchased goods (Cat 1) and use of sold products (Cat 11).

---

## Why This Matters for Climate-Tech

If your customers report under CSRD, SEC climate rule, or voluntary frameworks (CDP, TCFD, SBTi), they must account for emissions across Scope 1/2/3.

If your product:
- **Reduces a customer's Scope 1** (e.g., heat pump replacing gas furnace) — they want quantified reduction
- **Reduces a customer's Scope 2** (e.g., on-site solar) — same
- **Reduces a customer's Scope 3** (e.g., low-carbon supplier) — must be claimable in their inventory

Selling "we reduce emissions" is not enough. You need to specify: which scope, what methodology, what verification.

## Common Methodologies

For climate-tech companies generating credits or claims:

**Voluntary Carbon Markets:**
- **Verra (VCS)** — largest volume, broad methodology library
- **Gold Standard** — strong on co-benefits, sustainable development
- **American Carbon Registry (ACR)** — North American focus
- **Climate Action Reserve (CAR)** — California compliance focus
- **Puro.earth** — engineered CDR specifically
- **Isometric** — engineered CDR with rigorous transparency

**Compliance markets:**
- **EU ETS (Emissions Trading System)** — Europe's compliance market
- **California / RGGI / Quebec / etc.** — regional compliance markets
- **International (CORSIA)** — aviation

**Sector-specific:**
- **LCFS (Low Carbon Fuel Standard)** in CA / OR / WA / BC
- **Renewable Fuel Standard (RFS)** in US

For credit-issuing climate-tech, methodology choice is a business decision: which registry's methodology you align to determines who buys your credits and at what price.

## Measurement, Reporting, Verification (MRV)

MRV is the framework around climate claims:

- **Measurement:** Quantifying emissions or removals using a defined methodology
- **Reporting:** Disclosing measurements in a standard format
- **Verification:** Independent third-party confirmation

For voluntary credits, verification is typically by an accredited verifier (e.g., DNV, Bureau Veritas, SCS Global Services, EPIC Sustainability) at submission. Annual re-verification often required.

For corporate inventories, "limited assurance" or "reasonable assurance" by qualified auditors is increasingly common (CSRD requires limited initially, transitioning to reasonable).

**MRV cost:** Verification can cost $20k-$200k+ per project depending on scope and methodology. Plan for it in unit economics.

## Additionality, Permanence, Leakage

For carbon credits and removals, three concepts dominate:

**Additionality:** Would this reduction / removal have happened anyway without the project?
- "Business as usual" baselines are often debated
- Some registries use stricter additionality tests than others

**Permanence:** How long is the carbon kept out of the atmosphere?
- Engineered CDR (DAC + storage in basalt): 1000+ years
- Forest carbon: variable; risk of fire, disease, harvest
- Soil carbon: contested; some studies show high reversibility risk

**Leakage:** Does the project just shift emissions elsewhere?
- Forest protection: did logging move to a different forest?
- Industrial: did the avoided emissions move to another facility?

These three (often called "ALP") are where carbon credits have faced reputational issues. Voluntary markets have evolved methodologies to address them, but not perfectly.

## Lifecycle Assessment (LCA)

Many climate-tech products require LCA to substantiate impact claims. LCA is structured assessment of environmental impacts across a product's lifecycle (raw materials → manufacturing → use → end-of-life).

Standards:
- **ISO 14040 / 14044** — international LCA standards
- **PEF / OEF** — EU Product/Organisation Environmental Footprint methods
- **Sector-specific PCRs** (Product Category Rules) for many product categories

LCA is **methodology-sensitive**: results can vary 2-5x depending on assumptions about boundaries, allocation, and impact categories. Transparent methodology disclosure is non-negotiable for credible claims.

LCA cost: $30k-$300k+ depending on complexity and rigor. Plan for it.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Treating Scope 1/2 as the whole picture.** Scope 3 is usually the largest category for most businesses; ignoring it makes the analysis incomplete.
2. **Methodology shopping.** Picking the methodology that gives the best number rather than the most credible.
3. **Static baselines.** Comparing today's project to 2010 baselines makes everything look good. Use current realistic counterfactuals.
4. **Avoidance vs. removal confusion.** "Avoided emissions" and "removed emissions" are different. Climate-tech often conflates them; sophisticated buyers don't.
5. **Forgetting scope alignment.** Selling a Scope 3 reducer to a customer that only counts Scope 1/2 misses the point.
6. **No re-verification plan.** Many methodologies require annual or periodic re-verification; budgeting for it from start is much easier than retrofitting.
7. **Marketing claims outpacing verification.** "Carbon negative" is a regulated-attention claim; FTC has acted on greenwashing.
