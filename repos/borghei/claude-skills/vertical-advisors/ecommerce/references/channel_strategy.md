# Ecommerce Channel Strategy

Each ecommerce channel has its own economics, customer profile, and constraints. Picking the right channel mix — and modeling each channel separately — is one of the highest-leverage strategic decisions an ecommerce founder makes.

---

## DTC Site (Owned Channel)

**What:** Your own Shopify / BigCommerce / custom site.

**Economics:**
- Highest margin (no marketplace take rate)
- Direct customer data (email, behavior, return patterns)
- Full control of brand experience
- High CAC (you pay for traffic)

**Best for:**
- High-AOV products (CAC math works only at higher AOV)
- Brand-driven categories (apparel, beauty, premium goods)
- Repeat-purchase categories (LTV pays back first-order CAC over time)
- Subscription-suitable products

**Watch for:**
- Rising CAC: Meta and Google ad costs have risen ~3x in 5 years
- Attribution: post-iOS 14, attribution is murky; rely on cohort analysis
- Conversion rate: industry median 2-3%; below 1% means real fit problems

---

## Amazon

**What:** Sell on the Amazon marketplace, optionally fulfilling via FBA.

**Economics:**
- Amazon takes ~15% referral fee (varies by category)
- Add FBA fees if using FBA (storage + pick/pack)
- Net: typically 25-35% of GMV goes to Amazon
- No customer data (Amazon owns the customer)
- Lower CAC than DTC (Amazon brings traffic), but rising due to PPC competition

**Best for:**
- Search-discoverable products (people search by category, not brand)
- Lower-AOV items where DTC CAC is hard to justify
- Brands that have already validated demand elsewhere

**Watch for:**
- Brand erosion: race-to-the-bottom pricing, copycat sellers
- Account suspension risk: a single complaint or Amazon system error can pause your business
- A+ Content / Brand Registry: invest in Brand Registry to reduce IP issues
- Vendor Central vs Seller Central: very different programs; choose deliberately

## Marketplaces (Walmart, Target+, eBay, Etsy)

**What:** Sell on a marketplace other than Amazon.

**Economics:**
- Take rates: Walmart ~6-15%, Target+ ~5-15%, eBay ~10-13%, Etsy 6.5%
- Customer data partial (some give email, some don't)
- Each has its own approval process and standards

**Best for:**
- Categories where the marketplace has strong audience fit (Etsy = handmade/vintage, Target+ = household)
- Brands diversifying away from Amazon dependency
- Specific product types matching the marketplace ethos

**Watch for:**
- Application/approval timelines (Walmart, Target+ are gated)
- Inventory feed management overhead
- Competition with the marketplace's house brand

---

## Wholesale

**What:** Sell to retailers, who sell to end customers.

**Economics:**
- Wholesale = MSRP × 0.40-0.55 typically
- Net 30 / 60 / 90 payment terms common
- Trade marketing investment expected (co-op ad funds, slotting fees, promotional support)
- No CAC per individual sale, but business development cost is real

**Best for:**
- Brands with strong DTC validation
- Categories where retail discovery still drives meaningful sales (food, beauty, household)
- Brands able to absorb wholesale margin compression

**Watch for:**
- Channel conflict: DTC and wholesale can compete; pricing must be consistent or differentiated cleanly
- Cash flow: net-60/90 terms means ARR-style cash flow (revenue recognized but not collected)
- Returns: retailer returns can be a meaningful drag

---

## Retail (Direct)

**What:** Sell directly through retail stores — your own pop-ups or fully-owned retail.

**Economics:**
- Highest physical-channel cost (lease, build-out, staff)
- Often loss-leading at first; must be brand-builder, not standalone profit center
- Can drive DTC site traffic (city pop-up → email signups)

**Best for:**
- Brands at $20M+ DTC scale considering brand-extension
- Categories where physical try-on / experience matters (apparel, eyewear, home, beauty)
- Specific markets where local presence drives outsized brand awareness (NYC, LA, London)

**Watch for:**
- Retail has its own discipline; don't dabble. Either commit or skip.
- Lease terms locking you into 3-5+ year commitments
- Staffing and inventory overhead on top of fulfillment

---

## Subscription (Across Channels)

**What:** Recurring delivery of a product (consumable, replenishment, curation).

**Economics:**
- LTV multiplier (predictable repeat revenue)
- Lower CAC efficiency required (you can pay more for a subscriber than a one-time customer)
- High churn risk if value isn't reinforced

**Best for:**
- Genuinely-consumable products (coffee, supplements, pet food)
- Replenishment products (razors, toothbrushes, contact lenses)
- Curation/discovery (boxes — though many curation businesses have struggled to retain)

**Watch for:**
- Misaligned subscription vs. consumption rate (people don't actually use the product as fast as they're getting it)
- Subscription fatigue: customers actively pruning subscriptions
- Pause flows are essential — if customers can't pause, they cancel

## Channel Mix Sequencing

Most successful ecommerce brands roughly follow:

**Phase 1 (Year 0-1): DTC validation**
- Single channel: DTC site
- Test product, pricing, messaging
- Hit ~$1M run-rate before adding channels

**Phase 2 (Year 1-2): Amazon expansion**
- Add Amazon as discovery / scale channel
- Brand Registry, A+ Content, careful pricing
- Risk: Amazon dependence

**Phase 3 (Year 2-3): Selective wholesale**
- Specialty / boutique retail to build brand
- Cautiously expand to mass retail if category fits
- Avoid spreading thin

**Phase 4 (Year 3+): Considered diversification**
- Marketplaces, international, retail expansion
- Each channel justified by its own unit economics

Skipping phases (e.g., launching simultaneously on DTC + Amazon + wholesale) almost always under-performs sequencing.

## Modeling Channels Separately

The single highest-leverage practice:

**Compute unit economics per channel.** A blended view hides the channel that's destroying value.

Per channel, compute:
- Revenue per order
- Variable cost per order (COGS, fulfillment, channel take rate, payment)
- Returns / refunds
- Channel-specific marketing/CAC
- Contribution margin
- LTV (90/180/365-day)
- CAC payback

Use the calculator in this skill, run once per channel.

## Common Mistakes

- **Channel-blended LTV.** Hides the bad channels.
- **Amazon-first or Amazon-only.** Dependency risk, customer data loss, brand erosion.
- **Wholesale before DTC validation.** Retailers won't carry an unvalidated brand profitably.
- **Subscription-default.** Forcing subscription on a non-consumable product creates churn pain without LTV gain.
- **Channel proliferation.** Five channels at $1M ARR usually means none are run well.
- **Pricing inconsistency.** Different prices on DTC vs Amazon vs wholesale-shelf signals chaos.
