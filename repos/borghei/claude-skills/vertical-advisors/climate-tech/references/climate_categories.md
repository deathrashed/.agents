# Climate-Tech Categories

Climate-tech is a portfolio of categories with different scientific bases, regulatory landscapes, capital intensities, and time-to-impact. Picking the right category is the foundation of strategy.

---

## Why Category Choice Matters

A startup making heat pumps and a startup building carbon-accounting software both call themselves "climate-tech," but they differ in:
- Capital intensity (heat pumps need factories; software doesn't)
- Time to impact (a heat pump abates emissions immediately; software enables others to)
- Verification regime (heat pump efficiency is testable; software impact is indirect)
- Funding sources (DOE for hardware; venture for software)
- Regulatory exposure (energy regulations vs. ESG reporting standards)

These shape company-building decisions across team, capital, and timeline.

---

## Category 1: Energy Generation

**What it is:** New sources of clean electricity — solar, wind, geothermal, advanced nuclear, fusion.

**Examples:** Sunrun, Bloom Energy, Commonwealth Fusion, Helion, Fervo

**Capital intensity:** Very high for hardware/projects. Software-and-services lower.

**Time to impact:** Years (manufacturing, deployment). Operational projects: immediate.

**Magnitude:** Highest absolute potential. US grid alone is ~25% of total emissions; cleaning the grid affects every other Scope 2.

**Funding:** DOE Loan Programs Office, IRA Section 45/48, project finance, equity.

**Watch for:** Permitting timelines, transmission interconnection queues, cost-curve dynamics (solar/wind already very cheap; new entrants need clear advantage).

---

## Category 2: Energy Storage

**What it is:** Batteries (Li-ion, flow, sodium-ion, others), hydrogen, thermal storage, pumped hydro, long-duration storage.

**Examples:** Form Energy, ESS Inc, Highview, Antora Energy

**Capital intensity:** High — manufacturing scale-up dominates economics.

**Magnitude:** Indirect but large — storage enables higher renewable penetration.

**Funding:** DOE, IRA, project finance once at scale.

**Watch for:** Lithium price cycles, supply chain (rare earths, mining), competing chemistries.

---

## Category 3: Transport

**What it is:** Vehicle electrification (BEV, HFCV), sustainable aviation fuels (SAF), maritime decarbonization, charging infrastructure.

**Examples:** Tesla, Rivian, ChargePoint, LanzaJet, Twelve

**Capital intensity:** Very high for OEMs; moderate for charging infrastructure; varies for fuels.

**Magnitude:** ~25% of US emissions; specific sub-sectors (aviation, shipping, heavy trucking) hard-to-abate.

**Funding:** IRA EV credits, infrastructure investment, project finance.

**Watch for:** Charging network economics (loss-leading common), fuel pathway certifications (CORSIA, LCFS).

---

## Category 4: Industry (Cement, Steel, Chemicals, Manufacturing)

**What it is:** Decarbonizing hard-to-abate industrial processes — cement, steel, ammonia, ethylene, glass, paper, etc.

**Examples:** Boston Metal (green steel), Sublime Systems (green cement), Synthia AI (chemicals), Solugen (sustainable chemicals)

**Capital intensity:** Highest — industrial scale-up requires hundreds of millions to billions per facility.

**Magnitude:** ~30% of global emissions; cement and steel alone are ~14%.

**Funding:** DOE Industrial Demonstrations Program, IRA credits, project finance.

**Watch for:** Long timelines (10+ years from lab to commercial), customer adoption (low-margin industries are price-sensitive), policy dependence.

---

## Category 5: Buildings

**What it is:** Heat pumps, insulation, smart HVAC, electrification, building materials.

**Examples:** Mill (heat pumps), Sealed (efficiency retrofits), Sunhammer

**Capital intensity:** Moderate.

**Magnitude:** Buildings are ~30% of US emissions. Per-building small; aggregated very large.

**Funding:** Utility rebates, IRA Section 25C / 25D, state programs.

**Watch for:** Workforce constraints (HVAC technician shortage), permitting variability, customer financing.

---

## Category 6: Food and Agriculture

**What it is:** Alternative proteins, regenerative ag, precision farming, soil carbon, methane reduction.

**Examples:** Beyond Meat, Impossible Foods, Indigo Ag, Rumin8

**Capital intensity:** Moderate to high (manufacturing for alt-protein), low for software/data.

**Magnitude:** Food systems are ~30% of global emissions; livestock methane alone is ~14%.

**Funding:** USDA programs, climate VC, EU CAP.

**Watch for:** Consumer adoption (alt protein has had setbacks), soil carbon verification controversies, agricultural commodity cycles.

---

## Category 7: Carbon Removal (CDR)

**What it is:** Removing CO2 from the atmosphere — direct air capture, biochar, enhanced weathering, ocean alkalinity, reforestation.

**Examples:** Climeworks, Heirloom (DAC); Charm Industrial (biochar); Lithos (ERW)

**Capital intensity:** Very high for engineered removal; lower for nature-based.

**Magnitude:** IPCC scenarios call for gigatons of removal annually by 2050. Currently kt-Mt scale.

**Funding:** Stripe Frontier, Microsoft offtakes, advance market commitments, 45Q tax credit (US).

**Watch for:** Durability claims (engineered: 1000+yr; nature-based: variable), additionality, verification methodology choice critical.

---

## Category 8: Software / Data / Measurement

**What it is:** Carbon accounting, MRV (Measurement, Reporting, Verification), ESG reporting, climate intelligence.

**Examples:** Watershed, Persefoni, Sweep, Sustain.Life

**Capital intensity:** Standard SaaS (lowest in climate-tech).

**Magnitude:** Indirect — enables customers' reductions.

**Funding:** Standard venture; faster path to revenue than hardware.

**Watch for:** Commoditization pressure as the category matures, regulatory dependence (CSRD, SEC climate rule drove a wave), incumbents moving in (Salesforce, Microsoft).

## Common Mistakes

1. **Picking high-capital category with venture-only capital plan.** Software-style capital can't get green steel to scale.
2. **Confusing "climate-themed" with climate-impactful.** Carbon-accounting SaaS is climate-tech only if it actually drives reductions, not just measurement.
3. **Underestimating verification overhead.** Many climate claims that seem reasonable fail rigorous LCA or methodology review.
4. **Policy-dependent business model with no Plan B.** IRA-dependent business models face risk if subsidies change. Build for value beyond subsidy where possible.
5. **Picking nature-based without verification clarity.** Soil carbon, forest carbon credits face significant challenges; pick verifiable methodology.
6. **Mismatched timelines.** Hardware company with 10-year time-to-scale on a 7-year venture clock is structurally mismatched. Consider grant + project-finance + later-stage capital instead.
