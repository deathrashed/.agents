# Naming Framework

A name is the most-used word in your business. You will say it on every call, write it in every email, ship it on every page. Pick badly and you pay forever; pick well and the name becomes a mnemonic for a category.

---

## What a Good Name Does

| Function | What it means in practice |
|----------|---------------------------|
| **Memorable** | Repeats easily after one hearing — short, distinctive, rhythmic |
| **Pronounceable** | Spelled the way it sounds; spoken the way it's spelled |
| **Search-friendly** | A vanity Google query for the name doesn't drown in noise |
| **Trademark-clear** | No live trademark in your product class, no obvious near-conflict |
| **Domain-available** | Reasonable TLD obtainable (the .com is nice but no longer mandatory) |
| **Future-proof** | Doesn't lock you into a single feature, market, or geography |

---

## The Elimination Filter

Run candidates through these gates **in order**. Anything that fails a gate is gone — don't try to rescue it.

1. **Length:** Drop > 12 characters.
2. **Hyphens or numbers:** Drop. They dilute brand and are friction over phone.
3. **Pronounceability test:** Read aloud. If you stumble or it has multiple plausible pronunciations, drop it.
4. **Spelling test:** Hear it for the first time — can you spell it? If not, drop.
5. **Bar test:** Imagine telling a stranger in a noisy bar. Will they remember it tomorrow?
6. **Connotation test:** Search for it. Any cringe-inducing existing usage in your or adjacent industries?
7. **Trademark test:** Search USPTO TESS (or your jurisdiction). Any live trademark in the same Nice class?
8. **Domain test:** Check at least 3 TLDs (.com, .ai, .io, .co). At least one needs to be obtainable at a reasonable price.

After this filter, you typically lose 80-90% of candidates. That is the filter doing its job.

## Naming-Pattern Playbook

| Pattern | Example | When it works |
|---------|---------|---------------|
| **Real word** | Apple, Stripe, Notion | Distinctive product, willing to defend trademark hard |
| **Real word + suffix** | Dropbox, Mailchimp, Zapier | Adds specificity; suffix becomes the brand handle |
| **Vowel-drop** | Tumblr, Flickr, Grindr | Modern feel; risks looking dated when the trend turns |
| **Blend** | Pinterest (pin + interest), Instagram (instant + telegram) | Tells a small story in the name |
| **Made-up word** | Kodak, Xerox, Spotify | Maximal trademark protection; high upfront branding cost |
| **TLD-as-suffix** | get.fast, hi.io, play.dev | Cute on slides; people still type ".com" by reflex — friction |
| **Acronym** | IBM, AWS, GE | Only works at scale you don't have yet — avoid for new brands |
| **Founder name** | Tesla, Disney, Bloomberg | Strong if the founder is public; otherwise opaque |

## Common Pitfalls

- **The unintended language collision.** "Mist" is a German word for "manure". Run names past speakers of your top non-English target markets.
- **The competitor neighbor.** Naming your company "Datalume" when "Datalogue" is a Series-B in your space invites permanent confusion.
- **The future-locking name.** "PetSitterApp" is great until you expand to dog walking, vet bookings, and pet insurance.
- **The pun that ages out.** Cultural pun names date themselves to a specific year (and not always flatteringly).
- **The unsearchable name.** Any common dictionary word will cost you SEO investment that goes elsewhere if you pick a distinct name.
- **The "we'll fix it later" rebrand.** Rebrands cost 5-10x what picking the right name costs upfront.

---

## When to Stop

Most teams over-iterate on names because the decision feels permanent (it is). Here's a stopping rule:

1. Generate ~200 candidates.
2. Filter to ~30.
3. Shortlist 10.
4. Sleep on the shortlist 24 hours.
5. Pick whichever still feels right after sleeping, that passes the trademark and domain tests.

If you've spent more than two weeks on naming, you're avoiding shipping. Pick something passable, ship, and rename in 18 months only if the name is actively hurting you. Most won't.

---

## A Quick Heuristic

Read your candidate name aloud, then immediately read it inside this sentence:

> "Hi, I'm calling from [name]."

If you wouldn't say that on a sales call without flinching, the name's not it.
